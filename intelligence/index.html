What is an RNS Announcement? Complete Guide for AIM Investors

Confused by RNS feeds? This guide breaks down Regulatory News Service basics, types, and why AIM announcements move prices faster than you might think.

Published 28 February 2026 · 6 min read

If you're investing on AIM, you've probably noticed the volume of announcements. Thousands land every year—trading updates, placings, acquisitions, director dealings. But which ones actually matter?

RNS stands for Regulatory News Service. It's the official platform UK listed companies use to disclose price-sensitive information. On AIM, the volume is particularly high: over 10,000 filings in 2025 alone.

The problem: raw RNS feeds don't distinguish between material signals and administrative noise. Investors waste hours scanning, while markets move before they finish reading.

The Basics: What Gets Filed on RNS?

RNS is mandatory for anything price-sensitive. Companies must file within minutes of an event. The result: a firehose of information across every AIM listing.

Access is free via londonstockexchange.com/news. You can also monitor through dedicated RNS platforms that syndicate the feed. But free access means no curation. You get everything—director changes, office moves, RNS amendments, and genuine valuation shifts all in one chronological soup.

Key RNS Types on AIM

Not all RNS are created equal. Here are the ones that move shares:

Signal Type

Trading Updates

Revenue guidance shifts, profit warnings, or positive surprises. On AIM, a missed profit target can trigger 20-50% selloffs overnight. Conversely, strong trading can rally a stock 30%+ in minutes.

Signal Type

Placings & Capital Raises

New share issuance. Critical detail: at what discount to market price? A 15%+ discount dilutes existing shareholders and often signals cash desperation. Watch the percentage issued—above 20% can trigger sharp declines.

Signal Type

Contract Wins & Customer Updates

Material revenue boosters or losses. A significant contract can validate growth; a lost customer can signal headwinds. Context matters—is it 10% of revenue or 50%?

Signal Type

Director Dealings

Insiders buying = confidence signal. Insiders selling = potential concern (though often tax-driven). Significant buys (especially near 52-week lows) often precede rallies.

Signal Type

Results & Financing Updates

Cash balance reveals runway. Debt restructures signal distress. Fundraising announcements flag dilution ahead. Burn rate calculations determine survival length.

Why AIM RNS Matter More Than Larger Caps

On FTSE 100, single announcements rarely swing valuations 30%+. On AIM, they can.

Small-cap valuations are built on fewer assets, thinner liquidity, and higher growth assumptions. A dilutive placing, missed guidance, or contract loss cascades through the entire valuation model in hours.

Real impact: An AIM stock trading at 50p can drop to 30p on a profit warning. A contract win can push a 20p stock to 35p. Markets move fast.

The edge? Investors who structure their RNS analysis—categorizing by signal type, impact-scoring, and surfacing key context—stay ahead of the market noise.

Manual scanning doesn't scale. By the time you've read 10 RNS announcements, the market has already repriced the material ones.

How to Spot Dilution Risks in AIM RNS – 5 Red Flags

Dilution destroys AIM returns. Learn the 5 red flags in RNS filings that signal shareholder value erosion—before the stock price crashes.

Published 28 February 2026 · 7 min read

If you've held an AIM stock for 6 months and watched your ownership percentage drop despite not selling a share, dilution just happened to you.

What causes it? Placings. These are newly issued shares, usually sold at a discount to current market price to attract buyers (investors, institutions, founders).

Example: Stock trades at 50p. Company places 1m new shares at 42p (16% discount). Existing 10m shareholders wake up owning 10m / 11m = 91% of what they owned yesterday. Valuation is diluted.

The kicker: if company issued shares at steep discount, it often signals cash desperation. Markets know this. Dilution announcements trigger selloffs 10-30%+ regardless of what the money is "for."

How to Calculate Dilution Impact

When you see a placing RNS, extract these numbers:

  • New shares issued (in the RNS headline)
  • Price per share (usually discounted 10-25% to market)
  • Total consideration (new shares × price)
  • Current share count (searchable via London Stock Exchange or financial data platforms)

Then:

Dilution % = (New Shares / Existing Shares) × 100

Rule of thumb:

  • 0-5% dilution = mild, usually absorbed
  • 5-15% dilution = notable; expect -5 to -15% move
  • 15-25% dilution = severe; expect -10 to -30% move
  • 25%+ dilution = distress signal; can trigger -30% to -60% crash

These are generalizations. Context matters—a 20% dilution to fund a game-changing acquisition plays differently than 20% dilution because the company ran out of cash.

5 Red Flags to Watch in Placing RNS

Red Flag #1

Discount >15% to Market Price

Steep discounts signal urgency. If a stock trades at 50p and they're placing at 40p (20% discount), buyers are betting on downside or the company needs cash badly. Expect negative market reaction.

Historical pattern: Capital raises with significant discounts often correlate with subsequent downward repricing.

Red Flag #2

Vague Use of Proceeds

RNS says "working capital" or "general corporate purposes"? Red flag. Strong companies raising capital detail exact deployment: acquisition, facilities, product development. Vagueness = lack of strategy or hidden cash burn.

Red Flag #3

Multiple Raises Within 12 Months

First placing forgettable. Second within a year? The company is burning cash faster than guidance implied. Third placing? Distress signal. Watch cash runway if >2 raises annually.

Red Flag #4

High Volume Relative to Market Cap

Small placing (2-5% dilution) on a £200m company = meh. Same dilution on a £50m company = material. Rule: if new shares are >20% of market cap, impact is structural.

Red Flag #5

Bridge Debt or Convertible Overhang

RNS mentions existing convertibles or bridge loans converting? That's future dilution on top of today's placing. Total dilution could be 30-50%+ when all convert. Markets price this in ahead of time—often triggering sharp declines.

Real-World Example: Capital Raise Analysis

When analysing a capital raise RNS, apply this framework:

Metric Green Flag Red Flag
Discount to market <10% >20%
Dilution % <10% >25%
Use of proceeds Specific, strategic Vague, "working capital"
Debt overhang None mentioned Convertibles or bridge debt
Raise frequency First in 24 months Second in 12 months

Market reaction: Raises with 3+ red flags typically trigger -15% to -40% selloffs. Those with green flags often rally or stay flat.

Top 7 AIM RNS Signals That Drive Share Price Moves

Not all signals are created equal. Learn the 7 RNS announcements that consistently move AIM valuations—and how to prioritize them.

Published 28 February 2026 · 8 min read

Every working day, hundreds of AIM RNS flood the feed. Most are noise. Some shift valuations 20-50% in minutes.

The investors who win are those who spot high-impact signals fast—before the market reprices. Slow readers miss the edge.

Here are the 7 signal types most likely to move AIM share prices, ranked by typical impact.

1. Guidance Revisions

Market impact: +10% to +40% (upside); -15% to -50% (downside)

AIM companies guide on profit, revenue, or cash. Revisions trigger repricing. An upside surprise (profit forecast raised) can rally a stock 20-40% in a single day. A downside revision (profit warning) crushes them 20-50%+.

Why? Small caps trade on growth assumptions. Those assumptions live in guidance numbers. Revise them and the entire valuation model recalibrates.

Pattern to watch: Guidance raised by established management = material rally. Guidance revised mid-year after earlier forecasts = distress.

What to watch in guidance RNS:

  • Magnitude of revision (5% vs. 50%?)
  • Direction (up vs. down?)
  • Reason given (market strength, cost control, headwind, etc.)
  • Full-year vs. half-year guidance

2. Dilution & Placings

Market impact: -5% to -40% (usually negative)

Shares issued at discount = ownership dilution. See the dilution guide for deep dive. Quick take: expect -5 to -30% moves depending on discount, volume, and cash burn context.

Steep discounts (>20%) or large volumes (>15% dilution) signal distress and trigger the heaviest selling.

3. Contract Wins or Material Customer Changes

Market impact: +5% to +30% (contract wins); -10% to -40% (major customer losses)

A contract win validates growth thesis. If a B2B company announces a multi-year deal representing 10-30% of projected revenue, markets often rally 15-30%.

Conversely, loss of a major customer can signal demand weakness and trigger sharp selloffs—especially if that customer was 20%+ of revenue.

Context matters: A £1m contract for a £50m company is material. A £1m contract for a £500m company is noise.

What to extract:

  • Revenue magnitude (£, % of projected total)
  • Duration (1 year, 5 years, ongoing?)
  • Exclusivity or optionality
  • Customer creditworthiness (blue-chip = lower risk)

4. Director Dealings (Buys)

Market impact: +5% to +25% (insider buys, especially near lows)

When directors buy their company's stock with personal money, they're betting on appreciation. Insiders have private information; their buys are confidence signals.

Large director buys—especially near 52-week lows—often precede rallies. Conversely, high-volume selling by insiders can signal caution (though tax-driven selling is common and less meaningful).

Recent pattern: Multiple AIM company directors bought significant stakes in recent periods, many near stock lows. Several have subsequently rallied 20-40%+.

What to look for:

  • Size of buy (£10k vs. £100k vs. £500k?)
  • Buy price vs. recent trading range
  • Director seniority (CEO > CFO > independent director)
  • Pattern (isolated buy vs. multiple directors buying?)

5. Cash Runway & Burn Rate Updates

Market impact: -10% to -40% (often negative; dependent on context)

On AIM, cash is survival. A company burning £500k/month with £2m cash has 4 months of runway. Markets hate this—it signals dilution or distress ahead.

Cash balance disclosures in half-year or full-year results reveal runway. Declining cash balances quarter-on-quarter, especially if paired with guidance misses, trigger selloffs.

Rule of thumb: If projected runway drops below 12 months, expect fundraising within 6-9 months = dilution ahead. Markets price this risk in early.

What to calculate:

  • Cash at period end
  • Cash burn per month (operating expenses - revenue)
  • Runway in months (cash / monthly burn)
  • Trending (improving or deteriorating?)

6. M&A or Significant Acquisitions

Market impact: -5% to +30% (highly dependent on strategic fit and price)

Acquisition announcements can go either way. Strategic buys (expanding addressable market, acquiring revenue) often rally. Expensive or poorly-explained buys trigger selling.

Key consideration: Is the deal funded by cash (reduces balance sheet strength), debt (increases leverage), or shares (dilutes)?

Pattern: Growth acquisitions funded by modest debt = typically rally. Large acquisitions with >50% debt load = typically sell-off.

Parse for:

  • Deal size relative to acquirer's market cap (10%, 50%, 100%+?)
  • Funding method (cash, debt, equity?)
  • Strategic rationale (clear or vague?)
  • Earn-out or contingent considerations (flag future payments)

7. Debt Restructuring or Financial Distress Signals

Market impact: -10% to -50% (often negative; distress signal)

Debt restructures, covenant breaches, or lender amendments signal financial stress. Companies restructure debt because they can't service it under current terms.

These RNS are heavily negative. Expect -20% to -50% moves, especially if paired with dilutive equity raises (which are the solution to debt stress).

Context: Debt restructures paired with large placings = "company is stressed, needs cash and debt relief." This combination typically triggers 30-50%+ selloffs.

Flags to watch:

  • Covenant amendments (lender waiving terms = bad news)
  • Debt-for-equity swaps (creditor converting to shares = distress)
  • Maturity extensions (kicking the can down the road)
  • Paired equity raises (debt stress + dilution = double hit)

Prioritizing Signals: Impact Scoring

Not all signals carry equal weight. Prioritization framework:

Signal Type Typical Move Time to Reprice Frequency
Guidance revision (major) ±20-40% Minutes 2-4x/year
Profit warning -25-50% Minutes 1-2x/year
High-dilution placing (>20%) -15-40% Minutes 1-3x/year
Large contract win (>20% revenue) +15-30% Hours Varies
Debt restructure -20-40% Minutes 1-2x/year
Director buy (significant) +5-15% Days/weeks Monthly
Administrative updates 0-1% No repricing Weekly

The pattern: Guidance, dilution, debt, and profit warnings trigger immediate repricing (minutes). Contract wins take hours/days. Director buys accumulate over time. Admin updates are noise.

AIM Investing Mistakes: Ignoring RNS Noise and Guidance Changes

Most AIM investors make the same 5 mistakes when reading RNS feeds. Learn what they are—and how to avoid them.

Published 28 February 2026 · 6 min read

AIM investing is hard. Liquidity is thin, information asymmetry is real, and valuation swings are sharp. But most losses trace back to the same root cause: investors treat RNS feeds as chronological, not impact-prioritized.

They read in order. By the time they reach the material announcement, the market has already moved. They miss the edge.

Here are the 5 biggest mistakes—and how to fix them.

Mistake #1: Chronological Over Impact-Based Reading

Raw RNS feeds display newest first. On any given day: 40-50 new announcements across AIM. You start reading. By announcement #30, you've spent 90 minutes and skimmed just 60% of volume.

Meanwhile, the profit warning buried at #17 has already crashed the market 25%. You missed it.

The fix: Impact-score every announcement before reading. Profit warnings, major dilution, guidance revisions, and debt restructures get priority. Admin updates get skipped.

Structuring your workflow by impact reduces time-to-decision from 2 hours to 15 minutes and catches material signals first.

Mistake #2: Treating All Guidance the Same

"Company updates FY26 guidance" could mean anything. Guidance raised 2%? Barely material. Guidance cut 40%? Distress signal.

Most investors skim the headline, miss the magnitude, and don't react fast enough. Markets reprice the cut before they process the RNS.

The fix: Parse guidance RNS for magnitude of revision, not just direction.

  • 0-5% revision = noise
  • 5-15% revision = material; expect 5-10% move
  • 15-30% revision = significant; expect 15-30% move
  • 30%+ revision = distress; expect 30-50%+ move

Same for direction. An upgraded guidance (profitable to higher profit) is bullish but less dramatic than a downgrade (profit forecast to loss forecast).

Mistake #3: Ignoring Dilution Context in Placings

"Company places 500k shares at 45p" lands in your feed. You scan it: "£225k raised." Seems small.

But if the company has 2m shares outstanding, that's 25% dilution at a 20% discount. Markets will punish this 20-40%. You didn't do the math and missed the signal.

The fix: Always calculate dilution % when you see a placing RNS.

  • New shares / existing shares = % dilution
  • Offering price vs. last close = discount %
  • Both >15% = expect -15-30% move

Takes 30 seconds. It's the difference between catching the move and being blindsided.

Mistake #4: Missing Cash Runway in Results

Quarterly results land. Headline revenue is up 10%. You're bullish. You miss the cash balance: down 40% YoY. Runway: 6 months.

Fundraising is coming. Dilution is coming. The market realizes this days later and sells off 20-30%. You didn't connect the dots.

The fix: Build a simple cash runway table for every holding:

Period Cash Balance Monthly Burn Runway (months)
Q4 2025 £3.5m £500k 7 months
Q1 2026 (est.) £3.0m £500k 6 months
Q2 2026 (est.) £2.5m £500k 5 months

When runway drops below 12 months, set an alert. Fundraising RNS (and dilution) is coming. Position yourself before the announcement.

Mistake #5: Reacting to Noise Instead of Signal

"Change of Company Secretary." "Office address update." "Notice of AGM." These RNS dominate feed volume. Most retail investors waste time on them.

Result: They're scanning noise while material signals go unread. Classic misdirection of effort.

The fix: Use signal categories to filter. Ignore:

  • Administrative changes (officers, addresses, notices)
  • Regulatory housekeeping (PDMR updates, waivers of share dealing restrictions)
  • Non-material amendments to previous RNS

Read:

  • Guidance and trading updates
  • Capital raises and dilution
  • Contract wins and customer changes
  • Director dealings (significant buys)
  • Results and cash positions
  • M&A and acquisitions
  • Debt and financing changes

Signal filtering cuts signal-to-noise ratio from 1:10 to 1:1. You spend 10x less time and catch 100x more alpha.

The Common Thread

These 5 mistakes share a theme: manual processing doesn't scale.

When you're reading RNS by hand, you're optimizing for completeness ("I've read everything") instead of speed and signal detection ("I caught the material moves first").

The market doesn't reward thoroughness. It rewards speed.

The edge: Structured, categorized, impact-scored RNS analysis beats manual chronological reading every single time. You're not trying to be perfect. You're trying to be first to the material signal.

Free RNS Platforms

What it does: Official RNS feed. All AIM announcements posted here first. Free, official, complete.

Strengths:

  • Authoritative source

Weaknesses:

  • Chronological only

Best for: Researchers who need authoritative documents. Not suited for real-time trading.

Interactive Investor (ii.co.uk)
