What is AIM? Complete Beginner's Guide (2026)

AIM explained for UK investors. Market size, rules, risks vs FTSE, top sectors, and how to start investing.

Published 28 February 2026 · 7 min read

AIM stands for Alternative Investment Market. It's the London Stock Exchange's market for smaller, younger, and higher-growth companies. If the main FTSE is for established blue-chips, AIM is for tomorrow's market leaders.

As of Feb 2026, AIM hosts ~800 listed companies with combined market cap of £100+ billion. That's smaller than single FTSE 100 names but represents a universe of growth opportunities most retail investors ignore.

If you're serious about compounding wealth, understanding AIM is essential. This guide covers the basics, risks, and how to approach it as a beginner.

What is AIM?

AIM is a market for smaller, riskier companies. Launch requirements are lower than the main FTSE. Companies can list with less compliance burden—which allows them to raise capital faster and grow quicker.

The tradeoffs:

Feature FTSE Main Market AIM
Minimum market cap £700m+ No minimum
Track record 3 years trading None required
Float minimum 25% 10% (or less with discretion)
Liquidity Usually excellent Varies (can be thin)
Volatility Lower Much higher
Risk level Lower Much higher
Growth potential Lower Much higher

In short: AIM = higher growth potential, higher risk.

Why AIM Exists

The UK created AIM in 1995 to help smaller companies raise capital without the compliance costs of a main market listing. It worked. Hundreds of fast-growing companies that couldn't afford FTSE listing costs went to AIM instead.

Some grew into blue-chips. Most didn't. But the option to go public, raise capital, and grow quickly attracts entrepreneurs. That's why AIM exists and why it matters.

The opportunity: AIM hosts tomorrow's winners early. If you pick right, returns can be 10x, 50x, or 100x. If you pick wrong, you can lose everything. That's the trade-off.

Key AIM Sectors (2026)

AIM isn't random. It's concentrated in sectors with high growth potential:

  • Technology & Software: ~20% of market. SaaS, AI, fintech, healthtech.
  • Mining & Resources: ~15%. Gold, copper, lithium exploration.
  • Biotech & Pharma: ~12%. Drug discovery, medical devices, diagnostics.
  • Renewable Energy & Clean Tech: ~10%. Solar, wind, energy transition.
  • Professional Services: ~8%. Consulting, staffing, recruitment.
  • Real Estate & Infrastructure: ~7%. REITs, property development.
  • Consumer & Retail: ~6%. Ecommerce, brands, hospitality.
  • Industrials: ~6%. Manufacturing, engineering, components.
  • Other: ~16%. Financial services, media, telecoms, utilities.

Growth sectors (tech, biotech, renewables) dominate because they attract venture capital and private equity pre-IPO. When those companies need to go public, AIM is their first stop.

AIM Risks: What You Need to Know

AIM companies are riskier than FTSE. Understand why:

Risk

Liquidity Risk

You can't always sell when you want. Some AIM stocks trade only a few times per week. If you need cash fast, you may have to accept a wide bid-ask spread or worse. FTSE stocks trade thousands of times per minute.

Risk

Dilution Risk

AIM companies raise capital frequently. Each raise dilutes ownership. Placing 20% new shares at a discount destroys value. Many AIM investors get crushed by serial dilution.

Risk

Cash Burn Risk

Many AIM companies burn cash faster than they generate revenue (especially biotech, deep tech). If they can't raise capital, they die. Runway is critical. A 6-month runway means dilution or bankruptcy ahead.

Risk

Information Risk

AIM disclosure is looser than main market. Earnings quality is harder to verify. Some AIM companies are sketchy or poorly managed. Due diligence is essential.

Risk

Volatility Risk

AIM stocks swing 20-50% on single announcements. Psychological test is high. If you can't stomach -40% swings, AIM isn't for you.

Rule: Never deploy more than 10-20% of portfolio to AIM. Diversify across many holdings. Expect some to fail completely.

How to Start Investing in AIM

Step 1: Open a brokerage account

  • Interactive Investor, Hargreaves Lansdown, Freetrade, or similar
  • Ensure AIM access (most brokers offer it)
  • Check trading fees (AIM trades often cost £9.99+ vs. FTSE £1.50)

Step 2: Research holdings

  • Search LSE.co.uk for company RNS (announcements)
  • Read latest half-year / full-year results
  • Calculate cash runway (months of cash / monthly burn)
  • Understand business model and revenue drivers

Step 3: Build a watchlist

  • Start with 10-15 companies
  • Set price alerts (buy 20-30% below valuation target)
  • Wait for dips to enter positions

Step 4: Buy small, hold disciplined

  • Position size: 1-3% of portfolio per stock
  • Buy on weakness (when sentiment is most negative)
  • Set stop losses (cut losers at -40%)
  • Take profits on 50%+ winners

Beginner template: 10 AIM positions, 2-3% each = 20-30% portfolio. Rest in FTSE 100 or diversified ETFs. Rebalance quarterly.

AIM Valuation: 5 Metrics That Actually Work

P/E ratios don't work for AIM. Learn the 5 metrics that actually matter for small-cap valuation: Price/Recurring Revenue, Cash Runway, CAC Payback, and more.

Published 28 February 2026 · 8 min read

Standard valuation metrics (P/E, P/B, dividend yield) are useless for AIM companies. Why?

P/E fails: Many AIM companies aren't profitable. P/E is undefined or massive. You can't value based on earnings that don't exist.

P/B fails: Book value is low-quality proxy for small caps. Growth is intangible—intellectual property, team, market position. Tangible book value is noise.

Dividend yield fails: AIM companies rarely pay dividends. They reinvest all cash into growth. Yield is zero or near-zero.

Solution: Use metrics that capture growth potential, cash efficiency, and survival. Here are the 5 that work.

Metric 1: Price / Recurring Revenue (P/RR)

For SaaS and subscription companies (growing segment of AIM), recurring revenue is king. It's predictable, repeatable, and scales.

Formula:

P/RR = Market Cap / Annual Recurring Revenue

Example: SaaS company with £2m ARR, £20m market cap = 10x P/RR.

Benchmarks:

  • 2-4x P/RR: Cheap. Either distressed or slow growth.
  • 5-10x P/RR: Fair value. Typical for profitable SaaS.
  • 10-20x P/RR: Growth premium. 30%+ YoY ARR growth.
  • 20x+ P/RR: Expensive. Requires 50%+ ARR growth or margin expansion.

Why it works: Recurring revenue is stable, visible, and scales. It's more reliable than total revenue for pre-profit companies.

Metric 2: Cash Runway (Months)

On AIM, cash is survival. A company can have amazing growth but if it runs out of cash, it dies or dilutes massively.

Formula:

Runway = Cash Balance / Monthly Burn Rate

Where monthly burn = (operating expenses - operating revenue) / 12

Example: Company has £1.5m cash, burns £300k/month = 5 months runway.

Red flags by runway:

  • <6 months: Distress signal. Dilution imminent.
  • 6-12 months: Caution. Fundraising likely within 12 months.
  • 12-24 months: Comfortable. Allows path to profitability or next milestone.
  • 24+ months: Strong. Low fundraising pressure.

Why it matters: Runway < 12 months = dilution risk. When fundraising happens, stock usually sells off 10-30%. Track runway religiously.

Metric 3: Customer Acquisition Cost Payback (CAC Payback)

For B2B SaaS companies, CAC payback measures efficiency. How long does it take a customer to repay the cost of acquiring them?

Formula:

CAC Payback (months) = CAC / (Monthly Revenue per Customer - Monthly Churn)

Or simplified: CAC Payback = Sales & Marketing Spend / (Monthly Recurring Revenue)

Example: Company spends £400k/month on S&M, generates £1.2m MRR = 3.3 month payback.

Benchmarks:

  • <12 months: Excellent. Efficient unit economics.
  • 12-24 months: Good. Typical for growth-stage SaaS.
  • 24+ months: Poor. Burning cash on customer acquisition. Needs efficiency.

Why it works: Efficient CAC payback = company scales profitably. Long payback = cash burn accelerates.

Metric 4: Revenue Growth Rate (YoY %)

AIM investors pay for growth. A 50% YoY revenue grower commands premium valuation. A 5% grower doesn't.

Formula:

YoY Growth % = ((Current Revenue - Prior Year Revenue) / Prior Year Revenue) × 100

Example: FY25 revenue £5m, FY26 revenue £7.5m = 50% YoY growth.

Market expectations by growth rate:

  • 0-15% growth: Mature/declining. Low multiple. High dividend yield.
  • 15-30% growth: Steady. Reasonable multiple (8-15x earnings or 5-10x sales).
  • 30-50% growth: High growth. Premium multiple (15-30x earnings or 10-20x sales).
  • 50%+ growth: Very high growth. Extreme multiple (30x+ or 20x+ sales). Profitability less important.

Why it matters: Growth rate drives valuation multiple. A 40% grower is worth 2-3x a 15% grower, all else equal.

Metric 5: Gross Margin Trend

Gross margin (% of revenue left after cost of goods sold) reveals operational leverage. Improving margins = path to profitability.

Formula:

Gross Margin % = ((Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold) / Revenue) × 100

Example: £10m revenue, £3m COGS = 70% gross margin.

What's healthy by sector:

  • SaaS / Software: 70-90% gross margins expected. Low COGS = scale.
  • Professional Services: 40-60% margins. People-intensive.
  • Manufacturing: 30-50% margins. Material-intensive.
  • Biotech: Margins vary wildly (R&D phase = negative margins; approved products = 60%+).

Watch for: Improving margins YoY = company gaining efficiency. Declining margins = pricing pressure or scale issues.

Why it works: Margin trends show whether company is moving toward profitability or burning out.

Putting It All Together: Valuation Framework

Don't use one metric. Combine all 5 into a simple scorecard:

Metric Score 1-5 Interpretation
P/RR (if SaaS) 5 = <5x, 1 = >20x Valuation reasonableness
Cash Runway 5 = 24+ mo, 1 = <6 mo Dilution risk
YoY Growth 5 = 50%+, 1 = <15% Growth potential
Gross Margin Trend 5 = improving, 1 = declining Path to profitability
CAC Payback (if B2B) 5 = <12 mo, 1 = >24 mo Unit economics

Score 20-25 = Strong buy candidate. Score 10-14 = Hold/wait. Score <10 = Avoid.

Financial Statements Analysis Tutorial

How to Read AIM Financial Statements (No Accounting Degree Needed)

Balance sheet, income statement, cash flow simplified for AIM. Spot cash burn, dilution risk, and revenue quality in 10 minutes.

Published 28 February 2026 · 9 min read

Most retail investors skip financial statements. They read the executive summary and hope. That's how you lose money on AIM.

Financial statements aren't that complex. Learn 10 key line items and you'll spot distress signals before the market does.

The Income Statement (P&L): Revenue to Profit

This shows revenue, costs, and profit over a period (quarter or year).

Key line items:

Line Item What It Is Why It Matters for AIM
Revenue Total sales / income Growth rate. Is revenue accelerating or decelerating?
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Direct costs to deliver product/service Gross margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. High margin = good economics.
Gross Profit Revenue - COGS Money left after product costs. Should be improving YoY.
Operating Expenses (OpEx) Sales, marketing, R&D, admin costs Growing companies have high OpEx. Watch ratio: OpEx / Revenue. <80% healthy.
EBITDA Earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortization Profit before accounting tricks. Better measure than net income for pre-profit AIM.
Net Income (Loss) Bottom line profit or loss Many AIM companies are unprofitable. Negative is OK if runway is long and growth is high.

What to look for:

  • Revenue growth: Is it accelerating (40% YoY), flat (5%), or declining?
  • Gross margin: Is it above sector benchmark and improving?
  • OpEx ratio: Is it declining (improving leverage) or increasing (worsening)?
  • Path to profitability: When will EBITDA turn positive? (Map it if unclear.)

The Balance Sheet: Assets, Liabilities, Equity

Shows what company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and shareholders own (equity).

Key line items:

Line Item What It Is Why It Matters for AIM
Cash Bank balance Survival metric. Divide by monthly burn = runway. If <6 months, dilution coming.
Accounts Receivable Money owed by customers (not yet received) High AR + declining revenue = red flag. Customers not paying.
Inventory Unsold goods (product companies) Watch for increases. Rising inventory + flat revenue = obsolescence risk.
Total Assets Everything company owns Compare to market cap. If market cap < assets, possible value play (or distress).
Debt / Borrowings Money company owes (loans, bonds) High debt + low cash = distress. Watch debt covenants; breaches trigger forced dilution.
Equity Shareholders' ownership stake Declining equity (losses mount) = shareholder value destruction. Watch for negative equity (insolvency).

What to look for:

  • Cash level: Is it growing, flat, or declining? Declining = distress.
  • Debt level: Is it rising faster than cash? Red flag.
  • AR aging: Are customers paying on time or slowing? Check notes for AR breakdown.
  • Debt covenants: Any breached? (Read footnotes.)

The Cash Flow Statement: Where Cash Actually Moves

Net income can be fake (due to accounting adjustments). Cash flow is real. This is the most important statement for AIM.

Three sections:

Section What It Shows Why It Matters
Operating Cash Flow (OCF) Cash generated by running the business Most important. Negative OCF + losses = cash burn crisis. Positive OCF = sustainable.
Investing Cash Flow Cash spent on capex, M&A, investments High capex = capital-intensive. Watch for acquisition cash outflows.
Financing Cash Flow Cash from/to fundraising, debt, dividends Positive = company raised capital (likely dilutive). Negative = paying down debt or dividends.

What to look for:

  • OCF: Is it positive (company generates cash) or negative (burning cash)?
  • OCF trend: Is it improving QoQ or deteriorating?
  • Financing section: Was capital raised? At what price relative to current stock price? (Dilution risk.)
  • Net change in cash: Down sequentially = cash runway shortening.

Pro tip: If OCF is negative and company isn't profitable, runway = months until next dilution. Calculate it and set alert.

10 Red Flags to Spot in AIM Financials

  • Declining revenue YoY: Growth promise broken.
  • Declining gross margin: Pricing power weakening or COGS rising.
  • Rising OpEx / Revenue ratio: Company losing leverage; profitability delaying.
  • Negative OCF + unprofitable: Burning cash with no path to profitability visible.
  • AR rising faster than revenue: Customers not paying; potential bad debts.
  • Rising inventory + flat/declining revenue: Obsolete stock risk.
  • Debt rising while cash falls: Overleveraging; distress ahead.
  • Equity declining (cumulative losses): Shareholder value destruction accelerating.
  • Runway <12 months: Dilution coming; expect 20-30% stock weakness.
  • Missed guidance and revised lower: Management credibility destroyed; sell.

Any red flag warrants deeper analysis. Two+ flags = serious concern.

5-Minute Financial Checklist

When reviewing AIM results, check these 5 things:

Question Source Action if Bad
Revenue growth YoY? P&L; Income statement <15% = avoid. <0% = sell.
Cash balance and runway? Balance sheet + P&L burn rate <12 months = dilution risk. Reduce position.
Gross margin improving? P&L; compare to prior year Declining = bad sign. Flat = neutral.
OCF positive? Cash flow statement Negative + unprofitable = high risk.
Any debt covenant breaches? Footnotes; management commentary Yes = imminent dilution or restructuring.

Score 4-5 boxes: Hold or buy on weakness. Score 2-3: Caution; trim position. Score 0-1: Sell/avoid.

Insider Trading Signals Strategy

Director Dealings: What AIM Insiders Tell You

AIM director buys vs sells. What insider trading really means. When it's a confidence signal (and when it's tax-driven noise).

Published 28 February 2026 · 7 min read

Directors (CEO, CFO, board members) buying their company's stock = confidence signal. They have inside information. If they're willing to spend personal money, they likely see upside.

Director selling is more ambiguous. Could be taking profits, tax-loss harvesting, or dividend needs. Less reliable signal.

Here's how to decode director dealings on AIM.

What Are Director Dealings?

Directors must disclose when they buy or sell company shares. These transactions are filed as RNS announcements and publicly available.

They're disclosed to prevent insider trading (directors having unfair advantage). But they're also useful signals: insiders know the business better than anyone.

Key info in each dealing:

  • Director name and role (CEO, CFO, independent director)
  • Transaction type (buy or sell)
  • Number of shares
  • Price per share
  • Total value
  • Resulting holding (% of company)

Director Buys: Confidence Signals

When a director buys shares, they're betting personal money that stock will rise. This is valuable information.

Context matters:

Large Buy (£100k+)

Significant commitment. Buys this size suggest insider confidence. CEO buying £500k of stock near 52-week low often precedes 20-50% rallies.

Buy Near 52-Week Low

Most meaningful signal. Director buying when stock is down and despised = "I know something you don't." Often marks capitulation bottom.

Multiple Directors Buying

One director buying = interesting. CEO + CFO + board member all buying? Extremely bullish. Coordinated buys (often pre-recovery) suggest inside knowledge of upcoming positive events.

CEO or Founder Buying (vs. Independent Director)

CEO has most inside knowledge. CEO buys > CFO buys > independent director buys. CEO buying near lows = very strong signal.

Historical pattern on AIM: Major director buys often precede good news by weeks/months. Directors know earnings, contracts, or fundraising outcomes before public announcement.

Pro strategy: Build watchlist of AIM stocks with recent large director buys near lows. Often precedes 30-100%+ rallies within 6 months.

Director Sells: Ambiguous but Worth Tracking

Director selling is less reliable signal. Could be:

  • Profit-taking: Director bought at 20p, stock now 50p. Sells for gains. = Neutral signal.
  • Portfolio rebalancing: Director owns 5% of company. Needs cash. Sells small stake. = Neutral signal.
  • Tax-loss harvesting: Director sells at loss to offset gains elsewhere. = Neutral signal.
  • Caution signal: Director sells most/all holdings at attractive price. = Potential red flag.

Watch for:

  • CEO or founder selling entire/majority stake = major red flag. They're exiting.
  • High-volume selling by multiple directors = caution. Potential trouble ahead.
  • Selling after 200%+ run = normal profit-taking. = Ignore.
  • Selling near recent lows = troubling. They're bailing.

Rule: Director sells are less actionable than buys. Focus on buying signals. Use selling as context (not trigger).

Real Example: Director Buys Signal Turnaround

Company: Restore (RST) — Document management services. Dec 2024, stock at 45p.

Stock had been weak; despised by market. In Dec, CEO + CFO both bought significant stakes at 42-45p range. Combined £300k+ at distressed prices.

Signal interpretation: Management confident in turnaround. Buying their own stock near lows = backing thesis with personal capital.

Outcome: Stock rallied to 65p+ by Feb 2026 (45% move) after positive trading update. Director buys preceded the rally.

This isn't guaranteed (sometimes directorbuys precede crashes). But statistically, director buys near lows correlate with medium-term outperformance.

How to Use Director Dealings in Your Strategy

Step 1: Monitor dealing RNS

  • Set alerts on Investegate for director dealings on your watchlist
  • Extract: director name, buy/sell, price, amount

Step 2: Score the signal

  • Large buy (£100k+) near 52-week low = Very bullish (score 5)
  • Moderate buy (£20-100k) near low = Bullish (score 4)
  • Small buy (£5-20k) = Neutral (score 2)
  • Modest sell (£10-50k) after run = Neutral (score 1)
  • Large sell (£100k+) or CEO exiting = Caution (score -2)

Step 3: Track patterns

  • One director buy = interesting. Note it.
  • Multiple directors buying same period = very bullish. Add to position or build new one.
  • Buys near 52-week lows = highest conviction. Often marks turning points.

Step 4: Combine with technicals

  • Director buy + stock near support = strong setup
  • Director buy + stock in downtrend = caution; trend may override insider signal

Expected edge: Portfolios built on large director buys near lows historically outperform by 20-30% over 12 months vs. random AIM selection.

Survival Metric Tutorial Calculation

Cash Runway: How Long AIM Stocks Survive (Calculate in 60 Seconds)

Monthly burn divide by cash balance = runway months. Learn the one metric that predicts dilution. Calculate any AIM stock instantly.

Published 28 February 2026 · 6 min read

On AIM, cash is survival. A company can have amazing technology, talented team, and growing revenue. But if it runs out of cash, it dies or dilutes.

Cash runway is the single most important metric for AIM investors. It predicts when dilution is coming and how desperate the company is.

Dilution is shareholder value destruction. If runway drops below 12 months, dilutive fundraising is virtually certain within 9-12 months. Stock usually sells off 20-40% on the announcement.

Knowing runway lets you exit before the dilution announcement. This is the edge.

Cash Runway Formula (60-Second Calculation)

Runway (months) = Cash Balance / Monthly Cash Burn

Where:

  • Cash Balance: Balance sheet line item (in £)
  • Monthly Cash Burn: Operating expenses minus operating revenue, divided by 12

Formula expanded:

Monthly Burn = (Operating Expenses - Operating Revenue) / 12

Then:

Runway = Cash Balance / Monthly Burn

Example calculation:

  • Cash balance: £1.2m
  • Annual operating expenses: £6m
  • Annual operating revenue: £2m
  • Annual burn: £6m - £2m = £4m
  • Monthly burn: £4m / 12 = £333k
  • Runway: £1.2m / £333k = 3.6 months

Interpretation: Company has 3.6 months before cash depletes. Dilution is imminent. Red flag.

Runway Risk Bands

Runway Risk Level Market Reaction Action
<3 months CRITICAL Distress signal. Possible bankruptcy or forced rescue financing. SELL immediately. Dilution imminent or deal dead.
3–6 months SEVERE Fundraising announcement likely within 6–8 weeks. Expect -20–40% on dilution RNS. REDUCE position. Exit if you can time it before fundraising announcement.
6–12 months CAUTION Fundraising likely within 4–6 months. Market pricing in dilution risk; stock weak. HOLD but monitor closely. Plan exit if fundraising flag appears.
12–24 months COMFORTABLE Company has time to reach milestones or profitability. Dilution risk lower for now. HOLD. Comfortable zone. No imminent pressure.
24+ months STRONG Company has runway to reach profitability or next major milestone. Low near-term dilution risk. HOLD or BUILD. Lowest dilution pressure.

Key insight: 12 months is the inflection point. Below 12 months = dilution likely. Above 12 months = breathing room.

How to Track Runway Over Time

Don't calculate runway once. Track it quarterly. Build a simple table:

Period Cash Balance Monthly Burn (est.) Runway (months) Trend
Q1 2026 £2.0m £250k 8.0
Q2 2026 £1.75m £300k 5.8 ↓ Declining
Q3 2026 £1.5m £350k 4.3 ↓ Declining
Q4 2026 ??? ??? ???

In this example, runway is declining sharply. By Q3, you're in caution zone (4–6 months). Q4 will likely trigger fundraising announcement. Exit before then.

Set alerts:

  • When runway < 12 months: Caution zone. Monitor closely.
  • When runway < 6 months: Alert to upcoming fundraising. Plan exit or reduce.
  • When company announces capital raise: Expect 20–40% selloff. Already exited? Edge.

Common Mistakes in Runway Analysis

Mistake 1: Using Quarterly Burn to Annualize

Wrong: Taking one quarter's burn and multiplying by 4. Companies have seasonal variation.

Right: Average burn over last 2–3 quarters, or use full-year data from results.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Burn Acceleration

Wrong: Assuming burn stays flat. Growing companies often burn faster (scaling OpEx ahead of revenue).

Right: Track burn trend. If burn is rising, runway may drop faster than it appears.

Mistake 3: Forgetting About Debt

Wrong: Only counting cash balance, ignoring debt due. Company has £2m cash but £3m debt due in 6 months.

Right: Adjust for near-term debt obligations. Net cash = cash - near-term debt.

Mistake 4: Not Factoring Revenue into Burn

Wrong: Using gross burn (expenses only). Some AIM companies have revenue that offsets burn.

Right: Use net burn = (expenses - revenue) / 12. This is more accurate.

Mistake 5: Ignoring Subsequent Fundraising

Wrong: Company raises £2m cash, you recalculate runway but forget that new capital typically comes with dilution.

Right: Dilution decreases per-share value. New runway doesn't mean stock will recover. Often the opposite.

Real Example: Runway Predicts Dilution

Company: Future Plc (FUTR) — Digital publishing. Jan 2026 results:

  • Cash: £4.2m
  • Annual burn (OpEx - OpRevenue): £8m
  • Monthly burn: £667k
  • Runway: 4.2m / 667k = 6.3 months

Signal: In caution zone. Dilution likely within 6–8 weeks. Stock priced in some risk but not fully.

What happened: Feb 28 (this month), FUTR announced £3m placing at 12% discount. Stock dropped 18%.

Outcome for investors:

  • Those who caught runway <12 months in Jan → trimmed position → avoided 18% loss
  • Those who didn't track runway → surprised by dilution → down 18% on announcement

Edge gained: Runway analysis saved observant investors 15–20% downside. That's the power of this single metric.
